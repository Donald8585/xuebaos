# B. The 7 Core Tactics — Evidence-Backed Study Engineering

## English

### Tactic 1: Active Recall Over Passive Re-Reading

**What it is:** Active recall is the practice of retrieving information from memory without looking at the source material. Instead of re-reading your textbook or notes, you close them and force your brain to produce the answer. This can take the form of self-quizzing, blank-page brain dumps, flashcards, or simply explaining a concept out loud to an imaginary audience.

Passive re-reading, by contrast, is the most common — and most deceptive — study method. It feels productive because you're "covering material," but it creates only a shallow familiarity that collapses under exam pressure.

**Why it works:** Karpicke and Roediger (2008) demonstrated in a landmark study that students who used active recall retained approximately 80% of word pairs after one week, compared to roughly 35% for students who re-read. The mechanism is straightforward: retrieval is a memory modifier. Every time you successfully recall something, you strengthen the neural pathway. Every time you passively re-read, you strengthen only the pathway for recognition — which is much shallower.

The testing effect — the finding that retrieval practice produces better long-term retention than additional study — is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006). It's been replicated hundreds of times across diverse populations, materials, and settings. If you're not testing yourself, you're not studying — you're just reading.

**How to apply in XueBaOS:** Use the Recall Arena module. After each topic, close your notes and dump everything you remember onto a blank canvas. XueBaOS will compare your output against the topic's knowledge graph and highlight gaps in red. Target those gaps in your next review. Do this at the start of every session, not the end — the struggle of recalling before reinforcement is what builds retrieval strength.

**Founder's personal note:** "I wasted years re-reading. Years. The first time I tried a blank-page recall after a biology chapter, I could produce maybe 20% of what I thought I knew. It was humiliating — and it was the most honest feedback I'd ever received about my actual knowledge. From that day forward, I never re-read without testing first. The gap between 'recognizes' and 'knows' is the gap between a 3 and a 5**."

### Tactic 2: Spaced Repetition — Ebbinghaus to FSRS

**What it is:** Spaced repetition is the practice of reviewing information at strategically increasing intervals. Instead of cramming a topic once and moving on, you review it after 1 day, then 3 days, then 7, then 14, then 30 — with each successful recall, the interval extends. With each failed recall, it resets.

Ebbinghaus discovered the forgetting curve in 1885; modern systems like Anki's FSRS (Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler, developed by Jarrett Ye and the open-source community) use machine learning to optimize these intervals per individual user and per individual card.

**Why it works:** The neuroscience is clear: memory consolidation requires time and sleep. Cramming floods the hippocampus but doesn't allow for synaptic consolidation, which happens during slow-wave sleep over multiple nights (Rasch & Born, 2013). Spaced repetition times your reviews to interrupt the forgetting curve at its steepest point — right before you would forget — making each review maximally efficient.

Moreover, spacing creates "desirable difficulty" (Bjork & Bjork, 1992). The slight struggle of recalling after a gap strengthens the memory far more than easy, immediate recall. It's the difference between walking a well-paved road (re-reading) and bushwhacking through a forest (spaced recall). The paved road feels easier. The forest builds stronger legs.

**How to apply in XueBaOS:** XueBaOS's Recall Arena auto-schedules reviews using FSRS-like algorithms. Every flashcard, every concept, every diagram gets its own optimal interval. You don't need to track anything — the system tells you what to review and when. Just show up, do the cards, and trust the algorithm.

**Founder's personal note:** "I used to 'finish' a topic and never look at it again until the week before the exam. By then, I'd forgotten 80% and had to re-learn from scratch. Spaced repetition felt slow at first — I was reviewing old stuff instead of 'progressing' — but by month three, I was retaining topics I'd studied months ago while my classmates had already forgotten them. The tortoise beats the hare. Every time."

### Tactic 3: Memory Palace / Method of Loci

**What it is:** The method of loci is an ancient mnemonic technique where you associate items you want to remember with specific locations in a familiar physical space (your "memory palace"). To recall the items, you mentally walk through the space, retrieving each item from its location.

The technique dates back to ancient Greece (legend attributes it to Simonides of Ceos, ~500 BCE) and was the foundation of rhetorical training through the Renaissance (Yates, 1966). It's not a party trick — it's a battle-tested cognitive tool that competitive memory champions use to memorize thousands of digits, shuffled decks, and complex sequences.

**Why it works:** The method exploits the brain's exceptional spatial memory. Humans evolved to remember locations — where food is, where danger is, how to get home — far better than abstract facts. The hippocampus, which encodes spatial maps, is also central to episodic memory (Maguire et al., 2003, studying London taxi drivers found enlarged hippocampi correlated with navigation experience).

By linking abstract information to spatial locations, you're essentially "smuggling" facts into the brain's most robust memory system. Dual coding theory (Paivio, 1971) also predicts this advantage: information encoded both verbally and visually (spatially) has two retrieval routes instead of one.

**How to apply in XueBaOS:** Use the Palace Builder. Choose a familiar location (your home, your school, your commute route) and map concepts to specific loci. For biology, map the photosynthesis process along your walk from the front door to your bedroom. For history, map a timeline along your walk to school. XueBaOS's AI will help you generate vivid, bizarre, action-filled imagery for each locus — because boring images are forgettable images.

**Founder's personal note:** "I built my first memory palace for the DSE Biology respiration chapter. My kitchen became the cytoplasm, my living room the mitochondrial matrix, my staircase the electron transport chain. During the exam, I literally walked through my house in my mind and picked up each answer. It felt like cheating. It wasn't — it was 2,500-year-old technology that nobody bothered to teach me."

### Tactic 4: Mnemonic Story Chaining + Von Restorff Effect

**What it is:** Mnemonic story chaining involves linking unrelated items into a single, coherent (but preferably bizarre) narrative. Instead of memorizing a list of isolated facts, you weave them into a story where each element triggers the next through association.

The Von Restorff effect (Hedwig von Restorff, 1933) states that items that stand out from their surroundings — through bizarreness, color, emotion, or surprise — are dramatically more memorable. When you make your mnemonic stories weird, violent, sexual, funny, or otherwise emotionally charged, you exploit this effect.

**Why it works:** Story comprehension is one of the brain's most deeply wired capabilities. We've told stories for tens of thousands of years; we've been reading textbooks for maybe 200. A narrative structure provides a natural retrieval scaffold — if you remember the first element, the story logic helps you retrieve the rest.

The Von Restorff effect adds a second layer: bizarre images create prediction errors in the brain, triggering the release of dopamine and norepinephrine, which enhance memory encoding (Schultz, 2016). The brain pays extra attention to things that violate expectations. Use that.

**How to apply in XueBaOS:** XueBaOS's Story Generator takes your study content and creates narrative chains with adjustable bizarreness levels. Need to remember the reactivity series of metals? The AI generates a story about Potassium (K) the angry king who keeps exploding, Sodium (Na) his slightly calmer brother, and so on down to Gold (Au) the peaceful hermit who refuses to react with anyone. Use the generated stories or create your own — but make them weird.

**Founder's personal note:** "My chemistry reactivity series story involved a potassium atom getting into a bar fight, sodium throwing his drink, and gold just sitting there looking expensive. I can still recite the story five years later. The entire series. In order. I couldn't tell you what I had for breakfast yesterday, but I remember that bar fight because it was absurd. That's the Von Restorff effect in action."

### Tactic 5: Interleaving — Mix Subjects, Don't Block

**What it is:** Interleaving is the practice of mixing different topics, subjects, or problem types within a single study session, rather than studying one topic to completion before moving to the next (blocking).

For example, instead of doing 20 biology questions, then 20 chemistry questions, then 20 physics questions, you mix them: Bio-Chem-Physics-Bio-Chem-Physics. Similarly, within math, instead of doing all algebra then all geometry, you interleave problem types.

**Why it works:** Rohrer (2012) demonstrated that interleaved practice significantly improves long-term retention and transfer compared to blocked practice — even though interleaving feels harder during the study session itself. In one study, students who interleaved math problem types scored 63% on a delayed test compared to 20% for students who blocked.

The mechanism: blocking allows you to "hack" each problem by knowing which technique to apply before you even read the question. You're not practicing problem identification — you're practicing technique execution. But exams mix problems. The hardest part of solving an exam question isn't executing the formula; it's recognizing which formula applies. Interleaving forces you to practice that recognition skill.

**How to apply in XueBaOS:** Enable Interleave Mode in the QBank. Instead of studying one subject's questions sequentially, XueBaOS will serve you a mixed stream across your selected subjects. The algorithm tracks your performance per subject and adjusts the mix to target weaker areas more frequently. Start with 70-30 block-to-interleave ratio and increase interleaving as exams approach.

**Founder's personal note:** "This one hurt my ego. Blocked practice felt so productive — I'd do 30 chemistry questions in a row and feel like a genius. Then the exam came, with mixed questions, and my brain froze because I hadn't practiced switching gears. Interleaving felt chaotic and difficult, but after two weeks, my exam simulation scores jumped. The difficulty is the point."

### Tactic 6: Feynman Technique — Teach to Learn

**What it is:** Named after Nobel physicist Richard Feynman, this technique requires you to explain a concept in simple language as if teaching it to someone with no background in the subject. If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough.

The full Feynman Technique has four steps: (1) Choose a concept, (2) Explain it out loud or in writing as if teaching a child, (3) Identify gaps where your explanation breaks down or relies on jargon, (4) Return to the source material to fill those gaps, then simplify further.

**Why it works:** The Feynman Technique combines several powerful learning mechanisms. It forces retrieval practice (you're not looking at notes). It forces elaboration (connecting new knowledge to existing schemas). It forces simplification (identifying the core logic beneath surface details). And it provides immediate, honest feedback: jargon and vague language are symptoms of shallow understanding.

Research on the "protégé effect" (Chase et al., 2009) shows that students who expect to teach material learn it more deeply than those who expect to be tested. Teaching creates a sense of responsibility that drives deeper processing.

**How to apply in XueBaOS:** Use the Feynman Board — a blank canvas where you explain concepts out loud or in text. XueBaOS's AI listens and provides feedback: it highlights jargon that you're using as a crutch, flags gaps in your explanation, and suggests simplifications. You can also generate "student questions" — common misunderstandings that a learner would ask — and practice answering them.

**Founder's personal note:** "I discovered the Feynman Technique accidentally. My mum asked me to explain what I was studying, and when I tried to explain the Calvin cycle in simple Cantonese, I realized I couldn't. I understood the words but not the logic. That moment — realizing my understanding was surface-level — was the beginning of actually learning biology. Now I explain everything to an imaginary student. She's very patient with me."

### Tactic 7: Pre-Testing — Try Before You Learn

**What it is:** Pre-testing is the counterintuitive practice of attempting to answer questions on material you haven't studied yet — before you're exposed to the content. You take a test on a topic you know nothing about, get almost everything wrong, and then study the material.

Most students avoid this because it feels bad to fail. But that failure is precisely what makes it effective.

**Why it works:** The testing effect doesn't only apply after learning — it also applies before learning. Richland, Kornell, and Kao (2009) demonstrated that students who attempted pre-tests on new material, even when they got every answer wrong, subsequently learned the material significantly better than students who studied without pre-testing.

The mechanism involves two processes: (1) Pre-testing activates related prior knowledge, creating "hooks" for new information to attach to, and (2) The failure itself generates curiosity and attention — your brain pays closer attention during subsequent study because it wants to resolve the questions it couldn't answer.

This is sometimes called "errorful learning" or "productive failure" (Kapur, 2008). By failing first, you're priming your brain for deeper encoding.

**How to apply in XueBaOS:** Before starting a new topic in XueBaOS, take the Pre-Test for that module. It'll serve you 5-10 questions drawn from the topic's knowledge graph. You'll probably score terribly. Good. That's the point. Your brain now has specific gaps it wants to fill, and your subsequent study will be more targeted and more attentive.

**Founder's personal note:** "The first time I tried pre-testing, I scored 1/10 on a chemistry topic and felt like an idiot. I almost stopped doing it. But when I actually studied the topic afterward, everything 'stuck' faster. It was like my brain had already mapped out where the new knowledge needed to go. Now it's a non-negotiable part of every new topic. Fail first, learn faster."

### 戰術二：間隔重複 — 由Ebbinghaus到FSRS

**係咩嚟：** 間隔重複係以策略性增加嘅間隔去複習資訊嘅做法。唔係死記一個課題然後向前行，你喺1日後複習佢，然後3日，然後7日，然後14日，然後30日——每次成功回憶，間隔就延長。每次回憶失敗，間隔就重置。

Ebbinghaus喺1885年發現遺忘曲線；現代系統好似Anki嘅FSRS（免費間隔重複排程器，由Jarrett Ye同開源社群開發）使用機器學習去為每個用戶同每張卡最佳化呢啲間隔。

**點解有效：** 神經科學好清晰：記憶鞏固需要時間同睡眠。死記硬背淹沒海馬體但唔容許突觸鞏固，後者喺多個晚上嘅慢波睡眠期間發生（Rasch & Born, 2013）。間隔重複安排你嘅複習喺遺忘曲線最陡峭嘅點打斷佢——啱啱喺你將會遺忘之前——令每一次複習效率最大化。

再者，間隔創造咗「理想困難」（Bjork & Bjork, 1992）。喺一段間隔之後回憶嘅輕微掙扎，比起容易、即時嘅回憶，強化記憶嘅程度大得多。呢個就係行一條鋪得好嘅路（重讀）同喺森林度劈路（間隔回憶）之間嘅分別。鋪好嘅路感覺更容易。森林建立更強嘅腳。

**點樣喺XueBaOS入面用：** XueBaOS嘅Recall Arena使用類似FSRS嘅演算法自動排程複習。每一張記憶卡、每一個概念、每一幅圖表都有自己嘅最佳間隔。你唔需要追蹤任何嘢——系統會話俾你知要複習咩同幾時。只需出現，做啲卡，然後信賴個演算法。

**創辦人個人筆記：** 「我以前會『完成』一個課題然後永遠唔再睇佢，直到考試前一星期。到嗰陣時，我已經遺忘咗80%，要由頭再學過。間隔重複初頭感覺好慢——我喺度複習舊嘢而唔係『向前行』——但到第三個月，我保留緊幾個月前學嘅課題，而我啲同學已經遺忘咗佢哋。龜贏兔。每次都係。」

### 戰術三：記憶宮殿／位置法

**係咩嚟：** 位置法係一種古老嘅記憶術，你將想記住嘅項目同一個熟悉嘅物理空間（你嘅「記憶宮殿」）入面嘅特定位置聯繫起嚟。要回憶啲項目，你喺腦入面行過個空間，由每個位置提取返每個項目。

呢個技術可以追溯到古希臘（傳說歸功於Ceos嘅Simonides，約公元前500年），係文藝復興時期修辭訓練嘅基礎（Yates, 1966）。佢唔係派對把戲——佢係一個經過實戰測試嘅認知工具，競技記憶冠軍用佢嚟記住成千上萬個數字、洗亂嘅啤牌、同複雜嘅序列。

**點解有效：** 呢個方法利用咗大腦非凡嘅空間記憶。人類進化到去記住位置——邊度有食物、邊度有危險、點樣返屋企——遠遠好過抽象事實。海馬體，負責編碼空間地圖，亦係情景記憶嘅核心（Maguire等人，2003，研究倫敦的士司機發現擴大嘅海馬體同導航經驗相關）。

透過將抽象資訊連接到空間位置，你基本上係將事實「走私」入大腦最穩健嘅記憶系統。雙重編碼理論（Paivio, 1971）都預測咗呢個優勢：同時以語言同視覺（空間）編碼嘅資訊有兩條檢索路線而唔係一條。

**創辦人個人筆記：** 「我為DSE生物呼吸作用章節起咗我第一個記憶宮殿。我個廚房變成細胞質，客廳變成線粒體基質，樓梯變成電子傳遞鏈。考試期間，我真係喺腦入面行過我間屋然後執起每一個答案。感覺好似出貓。但唔係——係2,500年歷史嘅科技，但冇人教過我。」

### 戰術四：記憶故事鏈結 + Von Restorff效應

**係咩嚟：** 記憶故事鏈結係將唔相關嘅項目連結成一個單一、連貫（但最好怪異）嘅敘述。唔係記住一串孤立嘅事實，你將佢哋編織成一個故事，每個元素透過聯想觸發下一個。

Von Restorff效應（Hedwig von Restorff, 1933）指出：喺環境入面突出嘅項目——透過怪異、顏色、情感、或者驚訝——會戲劇性地更加難忘。當你令到你嘅記憶故事奇怪、暴力、色情、好笑、或其他情感上充電嘅時候，你就利用咗呢個效應。

**點解有效：** 故事理解係大腦其中一個最深層紮根嘅能力。我哋講咗故事幾萬年；我哋讀教科書大概得200年。一個敘述結構提供咗一個自然嘅檢索支架——如果你記得第一個元素，故事邏輯幫你檢索其他。

Von Restorff效應加上第二層：怪異圖像喺大腦觸發預測錯誤，引發多巴胺同去甲腎上腺素嘅釋放，增強記憶編碼（Schultz, 2016）。大腦會對違反期望嘅事物畀額外嘅注意力。利用呢樣嘢。

**創辦人個人筆記：** 「我嘅化學反應性序列故事涉及一個鉀原子喺酒吧打交，鈉掟佢杯嘢飲，而金只係坐喺度睇落好貴。我五年後仲可以背誦個故事。成個序列。順序。我唔可以話俾你聽我尋日早餐食咗咩，但我記得嗰場酒吧打架因為佢太荒謬。呢個就係Von Restorff效應喺行動中。」

### 戰術五：交錯練習 — 混合科目，唔好分塊

**係咩嚟：** 交錯練習係喺單一溫書環節入面混合唔同課題、科目、或問題類型嘅做法，而唔係完成一個課題先至去下一個（分塊）。

例如，唔好做20條生物題，然後20條化學題，然後20條物理題，而係混合佢哋：生物-化學-物理-生物-化學-物理。同樣，喺數學入面，唔好做晒所有代數然後所有幾何，而係交錯問題類型。

**點解有效：** Rohrer（2012）展示咗，交錯練習比起分塊練習，顯著改善長期記憶同轉移——即使交錯喺溫書環節本身感覺更難。喺一項研究入面，交錯數學問題類型嘅學生喺延遲測試攞到63%，而分塊嘅學生只攞到20%。

### 战术一：主动回忆 > 被动重读

**是什么：** 主动回忆是一种不看原材料，从记忆中提取信息的练习。不是重读你的教科书或笔记，而是合上它们，逼你的大脑产生答案。这可以是自我提问、空白页脑力倾倒、记忆卡、或者纯粹大声解释一个概念给一个想象中的观众听。

被动重读，相比之下，是最常见——也最骗人——的温习方法。它感觉很有生产力因为你「覆盖着材料」，但它只创造了一种浅层的熟悉感，在考试压力下会崩溃。

**为什么有效：** Karpicke和Roediger（2008）在一个里程碑式的研究中展示了，使用主动回忆的学生在一星期之后保留了约80%的词对，而重读的学生只保留了约35%。机制很直接：检索是一个记忆修改器。每次你成功回忆某样东西，你就强化了那条神经通路。每次你被动重读，你只强化了识别的通路——浅很多。

测试效应——检索练习比额外温习产生更好长期记忆的发现——是认知心理学中最稳健的发现之一（Roediger & Karpicke, 2006）。它已被复制数百次，横跨不同人群、材料、和环境。如果你不测试自己，你就不是在学习——你只是在阅读。

**怎么在XueBaOS里用：** 使用Recall Arena模块。每个课题之后，合上你的笔记，把你记得的所有东西倒到一个空白画布。XueBaOS会将你的输出跟课题的知识图谱比较，用红色标示差距。在下一次复习针对那些差距。在每一节的开始做这个，而不是结束——在强化之前挣扎回忆，才建立得到检索强度。

**创办人个人笔记：** 「我浪费了好多年重读。好多年。我第一次试空白页回忆一个生物章节后，我大概只产生到20%我以为自己懂的东西。很羞耻——但这是我收过关于我实际知识最诚实的反馈。从那天开始，我永远不会不测试就重读。『认得』跟『懂』之间的差距，就是3和5**之间的差距。」

### 战术二：间隔重复 — 由Ebbinghaus到FSRS

**是什么：** 间隔重复是以策略性增加的间隔去复习信息的做法。不是死记一个课题然后向前走，你在1天后复习它，然后3天，然后7天，然后14天，然后30天——每次成功回忆，间隔就延长。每次回忆失败，间隔就重置。

Ebbinghaus在1885年发现遗忘曲线；现代系统像Anki的FSRS（免费间隔重复排程器，由Jarrett Ye和开源社群开发）使用机器学习去为每个用户和每张卡优化这些间隔。

**为什么有效：** 神经科学很清晰：记忆巩固需要时间和睡眠。死记硬背淹没海马体但不允许突触巩固，后者在多个晚上的慢波睡眠期间发生（Rasch & Born, 2013）。间隔重复安排你的复习在遗忘曲线最陡峭的点打断它——刚好在你将会遗忘之前——让每一次复习效率最大化。

再者，间隔创造了「理想困难」（Bjork & Bjork, 1992）。在一段间隔之后回忆的轻微挣扎，比起容易、即时的回忆，强化记忆的程度大得多。这就是走一条铺得好的路（重读）和在森林里开路（间隔回忆）之间的分别。铺好的路感觉更容易。森林建立更强的腿。

**怎么在XueBaOS里用：** XueBaOS的Recall Arena使用类似FSRS的算法自动排程复习。每一张记忆卡、每一个概念、每一幅图表都有自己的最佳间隔。你不需要追踪任何东西——系统会告诉你复习什么和什么时候。只需出现，做那些卡，然后信赖算法。

**创办人个人笔记：** 「我以前会『完成』一个课题然后永远不再看它，直到考试前一星期。到那时候，我已经遗忘了80%，要由头再学过。间隔重复初头感觉很慢——我在复习旧东西而不是『向前进』——但到第三个月，我保留着几个月前学的课题，而我同学已经遗忘了它们。龟赢兔。每次都是。」

### 战术三：记忆宫殿／位置法

**是什么：** 位置法是一种古老的记忆术，你将想记住的项目与一个熟悉的物理空间（你的「记忆宫殿」）里的特定位置联系起来。要回忆那些项目，你在脑里走过那个空间，由每个位置提取回每个项目。

这技术可以追溯到古希腊（传说归功于Ceos的Simonides，约公元前500年），是文艺复兴时期修辞训练的基础（Yates, 1966）。它不是派对把戏——它是一个经过实战测试的认知工具，竞技记忆冠军用它来记住成千上万个数字、洗乱的扑克牌、和复杂的序列。

**为什么有效：** 这方法利用了大脑非凡的空间记忆。人类进化到去记住位置——哪里有食物、哪里有危险、怎样回家——远远好过抽象事实。海马体，负责编码空间地图，也是情景记忆的核心（Maguire等人，2003，研究伦敦的士司机发现扩大的海马体与导航经验相关）。

通过将抽象信息连接到空间位置，你基本上是将事实「走私」进大脑最稳健的记忆系统。双重编码理论（Paivio, 1971）也预测了这个优势：同时以语言和视觉（空间）编码的信息有两条检索路线而不是一条。

**怎么在XueBaOS里用：** 使用Palace Builder。选一个熟悉的地点（你的家、你的学校、你上学路线），将概念映射到特定位置。生物科：沿着你从门口走到睡房的路线，映射光合作用过程。历史科：沿着你上学的路线映射时间线。XueBaOS的AI会帮你为每一个位置产生生动、怪异、充满动作的意象——因为无聊的图像是会被遗忘的图像。

**创办人个人笔记：** 「我為DSE生物呼吸作用章节建了我第一个记忆宫殿。我的厨房变成细胞质，客厅变成线粒体基质，楼梯变成电子传递链。考试期间，我真的在脑里走过我的房子然后捡起每一个答案。感觉好像作弊。但不是——是2,500年历史的技术，但没人教过我。」

### 战术四：记忆故事链接 + Von Restorff效应

**是什么：** 记忆故事链接是将不相关的项目链接成一个单一、连贯（但最好怪异）的叙述。不是记住一串孤立的事实，你将它们编织成一个故事，每个元素通过联想触发下一个。

Von Restorff效应（Hedwig von Restorff, 1933）指出：在环境中突出的项目——通过怪异、颜色、情感、或惊讶——会戏剧性地更加难忘。当你令你的记忆故事奇怪、暴力、色情、好笑、或其他情感上充电的时候，你就利用了这个效应。

**为什么有效：** 故事理解是大脑其中一个最深层扎根的能力。我们讲了故事几万年；我们读教科书大概才200年。一个叙述结构提供了一个自然的检索支架——如果你记得第一个元素，故事逻辑帮你检索其他。

Von Restorff效应加上第二层：怪异图像在大脑触发预测错误，引发多巴胺和去甲肾上腺素的释放，增强记忆编码（Schultz, 2016）。大脑会对违反期望的事物给予额外的注意力。利用这个。

**怎么在XueBaOS里用：** XueBaOS的Story Generator将你的学习内容创造出叙述链，可调整怪异程度。需要记住金属反应性序列？AI会产生一个故事，关于钾（K）这个不停爆炸的愤怒国王，钠（Na）他稍微冷静点的兄弟，如此类推直到金（Au）这个拒绝跟任何人反应的和平隐士。使用产生出来的故事或自己创——但要让它们怪异。

**创办人个人笔记：** 「我的化学反应性序列故事涉及一个钾原子在酒吧打架，钠扔他的饮料，而金只是坐在那里看起来很贵。我五年后还能背诵那个故事。整个序列。顺序。我不能告诉你我昨天早餐吃了什么，但我记得那场酒吧打架因为它太荒谬。这就是Von Restorff效应在行动中。」

### 战术五：交错练习 — 混合科目，不要分块

**是什么：** 交错练习是在单一温习环节里混合不同课题、科目、或问题类型的做法，而不是完成一个课题才去下一个（分块）。

例如，不要做20条生物题，然后20条化学题，然后20条物理题，而是混合它们：生物-化学-物理-生物-化学-物理。同样，在数学里，不要做完所有代数然后所有几何，而是交错问题类型。

**为什么有效：** Rohrer（2012）展示了，交错练习比起分块练习，显著改善长期记忆和转移——即使交错在温习环节本身感觉更难。在一项研究里，交错数学问题类型的学生在延迟测试拿到63%，而分块的学生只拿到20%。

机制：分块容许你「hack」每条问题，因为你在读题目之前已经知道用哪个技巧。你不是在练习问题识别——你是在练习技巧执行。但考试混合问题。解决考试问题最难的部分不是执行公式；而是识别哪条公式适用。交错逼你练习那个识别能力。

**怎么在XueBaOS里用：** 在QBank启用交错模式。XueBaOS会在你选定的科目里向你提供混合的题目流，而不是顺序研究一个科目的问题。算法追踪你每个科目的表现，调整混合比例，更频繁地针对较弱的领域。由70-30分块对交错比例开始，随着考试接近增加交错。

**创办人个人笔记：** 「这个伤了我的自尊。分块练习感觉很有生产力——我会连续做30条化学题然后觉得自己是天才。然后考试来了，混合问题，我的大脑死机因为我没有练习过转换模式。交错感觉混乱和困难，但两个星期之后，我的考试模拟分数跳升。困难才是重点。」

### 战术六：费曼技巧 — 教人去学

**是什么：** 以诺贝尔物理学家Richard Feynman命名，这个技巧要求你用简单语言解释一个概念，像教一个完全没有背景的人一样。如果你不能简单地解释，你就了解得不够深。

完整费曼技巧有四步：（1）选一个概念，（2）大声或用文字解释，像教一个小朋友，（3）识别你解释崩溃或依赖术语的地方，（4）回去原材料填补那些差距，然后进一步简化。

**为什么有效：** 费曼技巧结合了几个强大的学习机制。它强迫检索练习（你不是看着笔记）。它强迫阐述（将新知识连接到现有框架）。它强迫简化（识别表面细节之下的核心逻辑）。而且它提供即时、诚实的反馈：术语和模糊语言是浅层理解的症状。

关于「门生效应」（Chase等人，2009）的研究显示，预期要教材料的学生比预期要被测试的学生学得更深。教学创造了一种责任感，驱动更深层的处理。

**怎么在XueBaOS里用：** 使用Feynman Board——一个空白画布，你可以大声或用文字解释概念。XueBaOS的AI聆听并提供反馈：它标示你用作拐杖的术语、标记你解释中的差距、并建议简化。你也可以产生「学生问题」——学习者会问的常见误解——并练习回答它们。

**创办人个人笔记：** 「我意外发现费曼技巧。我妈叫我解释我在学什么，当我尝试用简单普通话解释卡尔文循环时，我发现我做不到。我懂那些字但不懂那个逻辑。那一刻——发现我的理解是表面级——是真正开始学习生物的开端。现在我向一个想象中的学生解释所有东西。她对我非常有耐性。」

### 战术七：预先测试 — 学之前先试

**是什么：** 预先测试是一种反直觉的做法，在你接触内容之前尝试回答关于你还没学过的材料的问题。你在一个你完全不懂的课题上做测试，几乎所有东西都错，然后才学习材料。

大部分学生避免这个因为失败感觉很不好。但那个失败正是令它有效的原因。

**为什么有效：** 测试效应不止适用于学习之后——它也适用于学习之前。Richland, Kornell, and Kao（2009）展示了，尝试对新材料做预先测试的学生，即使他们所有答案都错，之后学习材料的效果显著好过没有做预先测试就学习的学生。

机制涉及两个过程：（1）预先测试激活相关的先备知识，为新信息创造「挂钩」让它依附，（2）失败本身产生好奇心和注意力——你的大脑在之后的学习中更加专注，因为它想解决它答不到的问题。

这有时被称为「错误学习」或「生产性失败」（Kapur, 2008）。通过先失败，你为更深层编码而预备了你的大脑。

**怎么在XueBaOS里用：** 在XueBaOS开始一个新课题之前，做那个模块的预先测试。它会提供5-10条由课题知识图谱抽取的题目。你很大机会拿到很差的分数。好。这就是重点。你的大脑现在有了它想填补的具体差距，而你之后的学习会更加有针对性，更加专注。

**创办人个人笔记：** 「我第一次試預先測試，在一個化學課題拿到1/10然後覺得自己是白痴。我差點不再做。但当我之后真的学习那个课题时，所有東西『黏住』得快了。就好像我的大脑已经标记了新知识需要去的位置。现在它是每个新课题不可协商的一部分。先失败，学得更快。」
