## Feature 1: AI Memory Palace Builder
### Headline: *Your brain is spatial. Use it.*

Your hippocampus — the brain region responsible for memory consolidation — evolved to navigate physical spaces. London taxi drivers literally grow larger hippocampi from memorizing 25,000 streets. This is what the memory palace exploits.

**What it does:**
- Paste any study material — notes, textbook excerpts, syllabus bullet points
- Our AI analyzes the content and automatically designs a spatial layout
- Rooms become topics, furniture becomes subtopics, objects become facts
- Walk through your palace in 3D or 2D view
- Assign colors, themes, and sensory details to strengthen encoding

**Why it matters:**
Building a memory palace manually takes hours of planning and visualization. XueBaOS does it in seconds. You spend your cognitive budget on what matters: *learning*, not architecture.

> *"The method of loci leverages the brain's extraordinary spatial memory — among our most ancient and robust cognitive systems."* — Maguire et al., neuroimaging studies on memory athletes

## Feature 2: Mnemonic Story Generator
### Headline: *The weirder the story, the stronger the memory.*

Your brain remembers bizarre, emotional, and vivid stories far better than dry facts. This is the *von Restorff effect* — distinctive items are remembered better than ordinary ones.

**What it does:**
- For every concept, XueBaOS generates a memorable, often ridiculous mnemonic story
- Stories incorporate action, emotion, exaggeration, and personal relevance
- Choose your style: academic, humorous, dramatic, or *absolute chaos*
- Stories are narrated in your chosen language (English, 廣東話, 普通話)
- Example: Learning the reactivity series? "Potassium Sam threw Sodium Calvin off the Magnesium Lion into the Aluminum Cat's Zinky Iron cage with Tinny Lead through Hydrogen Copper where Mercury Silver caught Platinum Gold."

**Why it matters:**
Roediger & Karpicke (2006) demonstrated that elaborative encoding — connecting new information to vivid mental imagery — produces retention rates 2-3x higher than rote rehearsal. XueBaOS automates the most cognitively demanding part.

## Feature 3: Symbolic Object Forge
### Headline: *Abstract concepts become concrete images.*

Try visualizing "photosynthesis." Now try visualizing "hegemonic stability theory." Some concepts resist imagery. That's where the Symbolic Object Forge comes in.

**What it does:**
- Transforms abstract concepts into concrete, memorable symbols
- Generates custom icons, objects, or characters for each concept
- AI Image Generation (Pro tier) creates actual visuals of your symbols
- Symbols are placed within your memory palace at optimized locations
- Supports cross-modal associations: sound, texture, smell descriptions

**Why it matters:**
Paivio's Dual Coding Theory (1971) shows that information encoded both verbally *and* visually is recalled at roughly twice the rate of information encoded through a single channel. Every concept gets both.

## Feature 4: FSRS Spaced Repetition Engine
### Headline: *The algorithm that knows when you'll forget.*

Forget rigid schedules. Forget guessing when to review. FSRS (Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler) is the most advanced spaced repetition algorithm in existence — and it's research-published, not marketing fluff.

**What it does:**
- Analyzes your personal forgetting curve — not a population average
- Predicts the exact probability you'll recall each fact on any given day
- Schedules reviews only when recall probability drops below your target threshold (default: 90%)
- Adapts in real-time: do better, reviews space out; struggle, reviews tighten up
- Parameters are continuously optimized using your actual performance data

**Why it matters:**
Anki's SM-2 algorithm (from 1987) uses the same intervals for everyone. FSRS, developed by Jarrett Ye and published in peer-reviewed research, reduces review load by **20-30%** while maintaining the same retention rate. You study less and remember more.

> *FSRS v5 benchmarks show ~0.95 area under the ROC curve for predicting recall — meaning it correctly predicts whether you'll remember something 95% of the time.*

## Feature 5: AI Timetable Optimizer
### Headline: *Sleep-respecting. Saturation-gradual. Human-first.*

Most study timetables are aspirational fiction. They ignore cognitive fatigue, circadian rhythms, and the fact that hour 6 of studying is dramatically less productive than hour 1.

**What it does:**
- Input your subjects, goals, exam dates, and available hours
- AI generates a timetable that respects:
  - **Sleep schedules** — no 3 AM study blocks unless you actually want them
  - **Saturation curves** — subjects rotate before cognitive fatigue sets in
  - **Interleaving** — mixing related topics for stronger long-term retention
  - **Buffer zones** — built-in catch-up time for when life happens
- Adapts when you fall behind — replans automatically
- Integrates with your FSRS review schedule
- Exports to Google Calendar, Apple Calendar, or printable PDF

**Why it matters:**
A 2014 study by Cepeda et al. found that optimal spacing between study sessions depends on how long you want to retain the information. Their formula: the optimal gap is roughly 10-20% of the desired retention interval. XueBaOS's scheduler bakes this research in.

## Feature 6: Deep+Wide Question Bank
### Headline: *Surface memorization is fake mastery.*

Knowing the definition of "marginal utility" is not the same as being able to apply it to an unfamiliar scenario. Most students discover this gap during the exam.

**What it does:**
- Generates questions at multiple cognitive levels:
  - **Recall** — "State the definition of..."
  - **Comprehension** — "Explain why..."
  - **Application** — "Apply this concept to..."
  - **Analysis** — "Compare and contrast..."
  - **Synthesis** — "How would you design..."
  - **Evaluation** — "Critique the argument that..."
- AI adapts difficulty based on your performance
- Pulls from past paper patterns (DSE, IB, A-Level)
- Identifies your "illusion of competence" blind spots
- Provides detailed explanations, not just correct answers

**Why it matters:**
Karpicke & Blunt (2011) showed that retrieval practice (active recall) produced superior long-term retention compared to concept mapping — even when students *preferred* concept mapping and *felt* they learned more from it. Your feelings about studying are often wrong. XueBaOS gives you objective feedback.
