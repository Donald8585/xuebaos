## Body

Here's a truth every student feels but few understand: your brain was not designed to retain abstract information. It was designed to remember spaces, faces, dangers, and stories.

In 1885, Hermann Ebbinghaus mapped what we now call the *Forgetting Curve*. Without active intervention, memory decays at a terrifying rate:

- **Within 20 minutes:** ~42% forgotten
- **Within 1 hour:** ~56% forgotten
- **Within 24 hours:** ~66% forgotten
- **Within 1 week:** ~75% forgotten

You're not forgetful. You're not stupid. You're fighting millions of years of evolution that optimized your brain for survival — not for memorizing the Krebs cycle or the causes of the First Opium War.

### The Asian Student Reality

Across Hong Kong, Singapore, Taiwan, and mainland China, students are drowning:

- **DSE candidates** face 4 core + 2-3 elective subjects with content volumes that would fill textbooks taller than a water bottle
- **IB students** juggle Theory of Knowledge, Extended Essays, and 6 demanding subjects simultaneously
- **A-Level students** cram 2 years of content into exam marathons that determine their entire future

A 2022 survey by the Hong Kong Federation of Youth Groups found that **48.6% of secondary students reported high to very high stress levels**, with academic pressure as the #1 driver.

Students respond by doing what they've always done: *study harder*. More hours. More highlighters. More passive re-reading.

### The Research Is Brutal

Dunlosky et al. (2013) evaluated 10 common study techniques. Their findings:

| Technique | Effectiveness |
|-----------|--------------|
| Practice testing | **High** |
| Distributed practice | **High** |
| Elaborative interrogation | **Moderate** |
| Self-explanation | **Moderate** |
| Interleaved practice | **Moderate** |
| Summarization | **Low** |
| Highlighting/underlining | **Low** |
| Re-reading | **Low** |

The techniques most students default to — highlighting, re-reading, summarization — are the *least effective*.

### Spaced Repetition Alone Isn't Enough

Yes, Anki and Quizlet exist. Yes, spaced repetition works. But here's the uncomfortable truth:

**Spaced repetition without encoding is just spacing out your forgetting.**

Before you can *schedule* recall, you need to *encode* the information properly. You need to transform lifeless facts into vivid, spatial, memorable representations. You need a **system**.

That system is the memory palace — a technique so powerful it's been used by memory champions for over 2,500 years. And the problem has never been the technique.

The problem has been the *effort* required to build one.

Until now.
