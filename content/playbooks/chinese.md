### DO DIFFERENTLY: What I'd Try

**1. Drill QBanks — Volume Is the Chinese Equivalent of Immersion**

Chinese, like any language, rewards exposure volume. The difference between a 4 and a 5* isn't a secret technique — it's thousands of hours of reading and writing. Since you can't go back in time and read more Chinese books as a child, you need to compress that exposure into your exam prep period.

My hypothesis: drill Chinese QBanks with the same intensity that physics students drill problem sets.

- Reading comprehension: 3-5 passages per week, full question sets, timed
- Classical Chinese: 2-3 passages per week, with dictionary, translating every character you don't know
- Writing: 1 full essay per week, timed (90 minutes), with self-review against marking criteria
- Listening + Integrated Skills: 1 full paper per week

This is aggressive. It's supposed to be. Language subjects can't be hacked — they can only be outworked. The QBank is your volume multiplier.

**2. Read 文言文 Widely — It's a Different Language**

Classical Chinese (文言文) is the single biggest score separator in DSE Chinese reading. Students who are comfortable with it score well. Students who aren't, don't. The problem: classical Chinese is effectively a different language from modern Chinese. You can't understand it by "trying harder" — you need vocabulary and grammar specific to classical Chinese.

My hypothesis: treat classical Chinese like a foreign language. Build a flashcard deck of high-frequency classical Chinese characters and their modern meanings. Read classical Chinese passages daily — not just the ones in your textbook, but additional passages from past papers, classical anthologies, and online resources. Use XueBaOS's Reading Vault to upload classical Chinese texts and get character-by-character translations.

Common classical Chinese features to master:
- 之 (possessive, object pronoun, "to go")
- 其 (possessive, demonstrative, modal)
- 而 (conjunction — "and," "but," "then")
- 以 (instrumental — "with," "by means of," "in order to")
- 於 (locative — "in," "at," "from," "than")
- 為 (passive marker, "to do," "for the sake of")

Master these six characters and you've unlocked roughly 40% of classical Chinese function words.

**3. "Good Sentence" Vault — Steal Excellence**

DSE Chinese writing rewards: content quality, organization, vocabulary range, and rhetorical device usage. These are learnable through imitation.

My hypothesis: build a "Good Sentence Vault" in XueBaOS. Every time you read a Chinese essay, article, or passage that has a sentence you admire — a well-structured argument, a vivid description, a clever rhetorical device — copy it into the vault. Tag it by function (opening hook, argument transition, emotional appeal, closing statement). Before each writing practice, review 10 sentences from the vault that match the essay type you're about to write.

Over time, you build a library of sentence patterns that you can deploy in your own writing. This isn't plagiarism — it's learning craftsmanship by studying masters.
