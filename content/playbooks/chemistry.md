# Chemistry — Post-Mortem Playbook (5 → Aiming 5*/5**)

## English

### Honest Assessment: What Happened

I scored a 5 in DSE Chemistry. The honest, slightly painful truth: I didn't drill enough. Chemistry has a unique profile among the sciences — it combines the memorization demands of biology (reactions, mechanisms, tests, observations) with the problem-solving demands of physics (calculations, stoichiometry, equilibrium). To excel, you must do both. I did the memorization well. I didn't do enough of the calculation drill. This playbook is my hard-earned lesson.

---

### What I Did Right (Keep Doing)

- **3-Pass annotation on all chapters:** Chemistry content maps beautifully to the 3-Pass method. Red pen for definitions and reaction conditions. Geometry for reaction pathways and industrial processes. Symbols for test observations (flame colors, precipitate colors, gas tests).
- **Memory palace for organic chemistry:** Organic chemistry — with its endless reaction pathways, reagents, conditions, and mechanisms — is the perfect memory palace subject. I built a palace along my walk to school where each location was a functional group and each step was a reaction.
- **Past paper exposure:** I attempted a wide range of past paper questions and was familiar with the DSE format and common question types.

---

### DO DIFFERENTLY: What I'd Change

**1. DRILL. Relentlessly.**

This is the single word that separates my chemistry result from what it could have been. DRILL.

Chemistry calculations — mole calculations, titration, equilibrium constants, enthalpy changes, rate equations — require speed and accuracy that only come from volume. Understanding the concept gets you to 60%. Drilling gets you to 95%.

My mistake: I'd do 5-10 calculation questions on a topic, understand the method, and move on. What I should have done: 50-100 calculation questions per topic. Until the calculations became automatic. Until I stopped making algebraic errors. Until I could see a mole calculation and immediately know the 3-step path to the answer.

Chemistry calculations are procedural. Procedures become automatic through repetition. There's no shortcut. The students scoring 5** in chemistry have done hundreds more calculation problems than the students scoring 5. That's the gap.

**2. Topic-by-Topic AI QBank with Mistake Log**

For each chemistry topic, complete the XueBaOS QBank exercises in sequence:

- Topic basics (definitions, formulas, concepts) — 20-30 questions
- Standard calculations — 30-50 questions
- Integrated problems (multiple concepts combined) — 20-30 questions
- Past paper simulation — full timed section

After each batch, review every error in the Mistake Log. Chemistry errors fall into patterns: forgetting to convert units (cm³ to dm³), mixing up exothermic and endothermic sign conventions, forgetting state symbols in equations, misidentifying the limiting reagent. Your mistake log will reveal your personal error patterns. Drill specifically against those patterns.

**3. Reaction Condition Flashcards Are Non-Negotiable**

DSE Chemistry loves asking about reaction conditions — temperature, pressure, catalyst, solvent, concentration. "What are the conditions for the Haber process?" "What catalyst is used in the Contact process?" "What temperature and pressure for ethanol production from ethene?"

These are pure memorization. There's no understanding shortcut. Make a flashcard deck of every reaction + its conditions. Drill it daily. These are "free marks" that students throw away because they "knew it but couldn't remember the exact numbers." In chemistry, "450°C" and "400°C" are different answers. Precision matters.
